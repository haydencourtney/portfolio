On Olympia Coffee Roasters:

    The Pacific Northwest is known for doing things well—our craft brewers concoct the finest ales, our computer engineers code the most elegant software (Vista notwithstanding) and our baristas pull the tastiest, most award-winning shots of espresso. Olympia now has another venue for this sort of single-minded devotion to perfection—Olympia Coffee Roasting Company’s brand new Capitol Boulevard Coffee Bar.

    Tucked away in the charming South Capitol neighborhood and brainchild of longtime local coffee guru Oliver Stormshak, the latest property in OCR’s expanding micro-empire brings together all the elements that make for a rewarding experience in Northwest black gold. The shop itself is handsomely decorated with minimal ornamentation and lots of finished wood, a majority of the seating is Italian bar-style and surrounds the work area, allowing the magic to be viewed at all angles. Although quite busy on the sunny Saturday morning of my visit, all the baristas (including Oliver, himself) were friendly, engaged and obviously passionate about what they are doing. When I requested a doppio of Kanzu Rwanda, the young man behind the La Marzocco gleefully exuded “Good choice!” and proceeded to pull an exceptional shot of espresso. Single-origin espressos are not a typical choice in even the best coffee bars, the fact that Oliver has chosen to include and prominently display this option is telling of Capitol Boulevard‘s intention of catering to customers seeking a experience that goes beyond the ordinary (not surprising for a company crowned roast Magazine’s 2013 “Roaster of the Year”).

    Likewise, the choice of brewed coffees was numerous, offering a little bit of something for everyone. Building on this emphasis on personal experience and dedication to quality, OCR eschews the usual route of pouring pre-brewed drip from large carafes and instead each cuppa is made to order, right before your eyes. What really impresses one about this operation is the unwillingness to sacrifice quality for convienence: if you’re willing to wait for the short minute and a half it takes hot water to filter through the uber-minimal, “pour-over” brewer, you are rewarded with an exceptionally clean, crisp coffee experience (this drinker typically enjoys dripcoffee somewhat luke-warm, but the Columbian Eleuterio Lasso brew was so excellent that it was mostly consumed before reaching that state). Of course, to-go options (as well as the availability of other typical menu items such as mochas, chai and teas) are not hindered and in fact the service is quite speedy, even with the steady stream of locals, students, professionals and families witnessed on this balmy Saturday. Part of this is the talent of the baristas and another part the highly-streamlined organization of the shop.

    With this blend of quality, expertise and local friendliness, the Capitol Boulevard Coffee Bar seems destined to become an Olympia institution. If you haven’t yet, cart yourself (and your friends) down to 2824 Capitol Boulevard and let Oliver and Company treat you to a one-of-a-kind experience.

For Oh! Sweet Nuthins:

Along with longtime Olympia freak-out staple Arrington de Dionyso, Derek M. Johnson was voted the “Best Experimental Act” by the readers of the Weekly Volcano for 2013. This is testament to both the power of his compositions and the fact that Derek is indeed exploring an untrodden musical landscape. With an electric cello and a small army of effects pedals Derek embodies the spirit of the “one-man band”, crafting ten-minute songs that collect layer upon layer of looped motifs that build to hypnotic crescendos before collapsing in on themselves. On the night of the 23rd the cellist ended his set with a captivating demonstration of this style: after carefully bowing out and electronically manipulating a languid drone Derek dropped his bow and began accompanying it with intricate pizzicato runs. The notes and bends were heavily reminiscent of Hindu ragas without descending in to parody and the whole thing led to a crushing cacophony that felt like the heady epiphany of a deep meditative session. When the noise broke only a nebula of the previous six minutes remained; Derek used this backdrop to finish his performance with a precision-played, melodic series of intervals. On a bill that included otherwise straightforward rock outfits, Derek held his own and left an real impression with his unique song crafting.

The Blue immediately turned the tone of the night from introspective to fun-loving. This five-piece is driven by two factors: an excellent, versatile rhythm section and two-guitar attack that lays down funk-inspired jams for singer Jessie Branch to use as a base for her soulful, commanding presence. Like the band’s music, Branch’s vocals are strong, sexy and demand attention. After belting out a couple of songs in her Winehouse-esque croon she addressed the audience directly: “Stand up, dance!”. The Midnight Sun’s theater seating isn’t exactly conducive to a dance party, but the audience didn’t need much convincing; one small invitation was all it took to get people to their feet. Booties were shaken. Many of The Blue’s songs are reminiscent of 90s ska (Save Ferris) and northern soul bands because of the emphasis on the upbeat, but with more of a funk backbone and without brass instrumentation or needless nostalgia. The high-water mark of their performance came with standout track “Fakehearted Life”, which employs a full-on guitar skank and walking bass line to produce a slinky, sensuous tune that is subdued enough to bring the finely conceived (and well played) percussive pieces to the front of the mix, as well as the aforementioned vocal stylings.

Headlining the bill was Olympia’s Fuzzy Math, a three-piece that wears both hearts and influences prominently on their sleeves. The three members of the band are obviously very accomplished on their hardware: bassist Cosmo Mailhot plays with both precision and ridiculous ease, it seems like he might have very well been born with a five-string in his hands. His self-effacing, deadpan humor is also an important element to a set that involved several guitar changes and computer reboots. Drummer David Gies is equally talented on the skins, manning a hi-hat in much the manner that a mother bird may diligently attend to her newborn chick—he also expands on his acoustic set, adding synth drumpad and other MIDI controllers to his arsenal. Western Washington University student and principal songwriter Dominic Jenkins is hot shit on the guitar, retrieving sounds from his Telecaster that are at times otherworldly, often very pretty and always tightly-wound with his rhythm section. Fuzzy Math are at their best on songs like “Flying Saucer”—instead of relying on straightforward verse-chorus songwriting or vocal-driven tracks, the band’s technical expertise and experimental nature make for a stunning performance: Dominic builds layers of vocal snippets and noise on an electronic looper in between his refined guitar work while Cosmo and David keep the high-flying atmospherics grounded with their solid grooves.

On 2060 Record's 'Ides of March' Record Release Party:

Lubec hails from the Rose City, its members arriving here from Richmond, Virginia in 2011. They describe their sound as “Scupt-rock”, a term that is actually quite apt in expressing the sonic punch of their live show. Big, heavy guitars carve out twisting songs that at times seem to change direction with artful effortlessness. Nick Laurich and Eddie Charlton share the responsibility of keeping the music incredibly tight, hard-hitting and unpredictable. Caroline Jackson’s airy vocals and pretty synth ornament the slabs of music in a way that underlines its buoyancy and provides counterpoint to the heavy handed two-guitar attack, leaving no sonic space overlooked. Like a modern-day Superchunk turned to 11 and heard through an aural prism, Lubec also draws influence from shoegaze, garage rock and the heavier moments of east coast labels like Jade Tree, collecting and synthesizing these sources in to a sound that is bigger than the 2060 performance space and suggests bigger things in this four-piece’s future.

Compared to Lubec, Olympia’s The Celestials are somewhat more subdued event, although this understates the fact that Guitarist/Singer Max Keena and his crew play a set that is equally high-energy as the previously mentioned Portlanders, but without the kitchen-sink engagement with your eardrums. Instead of solid chunks of monolithic sound, The Celestials give one’s mind space to wander and appreciate the swirling, reverb-laden psychedelic guitar work and sometimes unexpected chord progressions. Some of the synth-driven songs are bouncy, playful and downright danceable—on par with the electronic acts that were more typical of the 2060 showcase than the two guitar-based groups reviewed here. The Celestials released their first 4-song EP last June and in that short time have positioned themselves as an Olympia group which must be reckoned with (and seen), now with 2060 as a vehicle for their exploits. Stay tuned.
